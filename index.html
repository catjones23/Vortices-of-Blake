Blake's Vortex and Analogies of Reading in Milton: A Poem in Two Books


Introduction

William Blake’s epic poem Milton: A Poem in Two Books (1810) follows its titular hero on a quest to redeem his creativity, that after one hundred years in heaven, he realises he had neglected during his life on earth. Travelling through a vortex before falling to earth as a star, Milton enters Blake’s foot on landing and assumes a creative influence over him. This literal collision of poets, one of whom is the poem’s actual author, establishes the themes of creativity, imagination, literary legacy and history fundamental to the poem, as well as asserting its deeply self-reflexive ambitions. Made explicit in the poem’s preface, these ambitions are to throw off a creative and imaginative dependence on the classical examples of ‘Greek and Roman Models’ which Blake believes had come to dominate the contemporary literary landscape as the predominant source of inspiration and instead to renew through reading, an independent English imagination.

This dissertation will focus on Blake’s vortex motif, arguing that it functions as an extended analogy for the transformative process of reading. That just as the vortex transforms those who are sucked into it in Milton, texts similarly draw in and transform the minds that read them. Applying this analogy to various levels of language—beginning with words and fragments, then passages, and finally considering the entire long poem—the dissertation will consider content and form as working in tandem towards Blake’s twin ambitions to both depict his hero’s creative and spiritual transformation whilst stimulating a simultaneous transformation of imagination within the minds of his readers.
